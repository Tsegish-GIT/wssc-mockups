Understanding Your Water Bill

Your water and sewer bill includes several components. This guide will help you understand each section of your bill and how charges are calculated.

Bill Components

Your utility bill consists of the following main components:

Water Charges

Water charges are based on the volume of water used, measured in units (typically gallons or cubic feet). Charges may include:

  • Base Charge: A fixed monthly fee that covers basic service costs regardless of usage
  • Volumetric Charge: The cost per unit of water consumed during the billing period
  • Tier Pricing: Some rate structures charge different rates based on usage levels to encourage conservation

Sewer Charges

Sewer charges cover the cost of wastewater treatment and disposal. These charges are typically calculated based on water consumption, as water used generally equals wastewater generated.

  • Base Charge: Fixed monthly service fee for sewer infrastructure
  • Usage Charge: Cost calculated based on water consumption
  • Treatment Fee: Charges for wastewater treatment services

Additional Fees and Charges

Your bill may include additional charges such as:

  • Stormwater management fees
  • Infrastructure improvement charges
  • Late payment fees (if applicable)
  • Service connection fees

How to Read Your Bill

Your bill statement contains important information organized into several sections:

Section Information Included
Account Summary Account number, service address, billing period, due date, and total amount due
Usage Information Current and previous meter readings, total consumption, usage history chart
Current Charges Itemized breakdown of water, sewer, and additional charges for the current period
Payment Information Previous balance, payments received, adjustments, and current balance
Messages Important notices, alerts, or informational messages from the utility

Understanding Your Usage

Your water consumption is measured by a meter installed at your property. The meter reading is taken regularly (monthly, bi-monthly, or quarterly depending on your service area).

Factors Affecting Water Usage

Several factors can impact your water consumption:

  • Number of people in the household
  • Seasonal variations (lawn watering, pool filling)
  • Appliance efficiency (toilets, washing machines, dishwashers)
  • Leaks or running toilets
  • Outdoor irrigation systems

Average Household Usage

The average household uses approximately 3,000-7,000 gallons of water per month, though this varies significantly based on household size and habits. Your bill may include a comparison showing how your usage compares to similar households in your area.

Rate Structure

Water and sewer rates are established through a public process and approved by governing authorities. Rates are designed to cover the costs of providing service, including:

  • Water treatment and quality testing
  • Infrastructure maintenance and repairs
  • System improvements and upgrades
  • Environmental compliance
  • Customer service operations

Current rate schedules are available on our website and are reviewed periodically to ensure they remain adequate to meet system needs while remaining fair to customers.

High Bill Concerns

If you receive a bill that seems unusually high, consider the following:

  1. Check for Leaks: A running toilet or leaking pipe can waste significant amounts of water
  2. Review Your Usage: Compare current usage to previous months to identify unusual patterns
  3. Consider Seasonal Changes: Increased outdoor watering or guests visiting can increase consumption
  4. Verify Meter Reading: Check your meter yourself and compare to the bill
  5. Contact Customer Service: Our staff can help investigate billing concerns and arrange a meter test if needed

Conservation Tips

Reducing water usage benefits both your wallet and the environment. Simple conservation measures include:

  • Fix leaks promptly
  • Install low-flow fixtures and appliances
  • Take shorter showers
  • Run dishwashers and washing machines only with full loads
  • Water lawns during cooler parts of the day
  • Use rain barrels to collect water for outdoor use
